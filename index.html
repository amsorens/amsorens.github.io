Hello!

I am an Assistant Professor of Physics at the Facility for Rare Isotope Beams, a scientific user facility for nuclear science funded by the U.S. Department of Energy and operated by the Michigan State University. I am a theorist in nuclear physics, and my research centers on thermodynamic properties of dense strongly-interacting matter. These thermodynamic properties can yield key insights into the elementary mechanisms governing strongly-interacting systems which, while in principle described by the theory of quantum chromodynamics (QCD), are inaccessible — with a few exceptions in limiting cases — to direct QCD calculations.

Thermodynamic properties of any system are often summarized in the form of its equation of state (EOS) or its phase diagram. Determining the QCD EOS through studies of matter in extreme environments — such as supernova explosions, neutron stars and their mergers, and, in laboratories on Earth, relativistic collisions of heavy nuclei — can help answer the following broad questions:

  • Does QCD matter undergo a first-order phase transition at finite baryon density?
  • How does the isospin dependence of the EOS evolve with density and temperature?
  • How do the dominant nuclear matter degrees of freedom and their properties change as the thermodynamic variables are varied?

I aim to address these questions using collisions of heavy nuclei at relativistic speeds, which I model theoretically within semiclassical transport simulations. Since the simulations require various theoretical inputs, this research benefits from close collaboration with complementary efforts to constrain the EOS and other properties of QCD matter (e.g., neutron star studies or ab initio calculations). Devising the simulation framework is a team effort requiring efficient programming solutions to reduce the computational cost. Comparisons to experimental data always require extensive use of high-performance computing. Recently, the high computational cost of the simulations has begun to be addressed with machine learning methods.
